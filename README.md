## The Uncomfortable Truth About Ebola Forecasts

In September 2014, the CDC published a forecast that became famous—or infamous, depending on your perspective. Using data through late August, they modeled two scenarios for West Africa:

- **If nothing changes**: 1.4 million Ebola cases in Sierra Leone and Liberia by January 20, 2015.
- **If isolation reaches 70%**: Transmission would decline sharply; peak and fade by year's end.

By January 2015, the actual number? Approximately 8,000–10,000 cases across the region. The "worst-case" forecast was off by a factor of 140.

But here's what nobody said at the time: the CDC was right, and they were wrong, simultaneously.

They were right about the exponential trajectory. If transmission parameters held and no interventions worked, 1.4 million was mathematically sound. They were wrong about believing the trajectory would hold. Because it didn't.

---

## What Gets Predicted Well (And Why)

### The First 1–2 Weeks: Nearly Perfect

Modern epidemic forecasts are accurate at short time horizons (1–2 weeks ahead) with good probabilistic calibration, but become increasingly unreliable at longer forecasting horizons.

Why? Because in weeks 1–2, the dynamics are set. Transmission network, population density, healthcare capacity, case detection—these are facts, not variables. You can predict them the same way you predict a ball rolling down a slope. The velocity is known. The friction is known. The end is calculable.

For Bundibugyo, this means: my prediction of 900+ cases by mid-June 2026 (made on June 13) is likely accurate. The cases already infected on June 8 will show symptoms by June 20–22. The chains of transmission set in motion before June 10 will reach secondary cases by June 25. Within two weeks, the system behaves like a mechanism.

### The Regional Scale: Days 15–30

Here, models start to wobble, but not catastrophically. If isolation rates don't change, if healthcare capacity remains constant, if population movement follows historical patterns, you can still see 3–4 weeks ahead with 80–90% accuracy.

For Bundibugyo, this means my prediction that Goma will begin seeing exponential case growth by July is sound. The city is accessible, it's a healthcare magnet, and it's where patients go when rural clinics fail. This is inevitable.

## What Gets Predicted Terribly (And Why)

### The Moment Intervention Becomes Real

The 2014 CDC forecast assumed a fixed intervention trajectory. It modeled "what if 70% isolation is achieved?" as a branching point. But it couldn't model *when* that 70% would actually be achieved, *how fast* interventions would arrive, or *how quickly* behavior would change.

Models predicted that transmission would decline substantially by mid-January 2015 if approximately 70% of Ebola patients were placed into isolation or care centers, and if safe burials were conducted. If this 70% goal could be reached, it would "bend the curve," causing transmission to drop off substantially.

The real outcome? By December 8, 2014, Liberia's cases fell over 900,000 cases short of the CDC's worst-case scenario, while Sierra Leone exceeded CDC's prediction by nearly 5,000.

The system didn't behave uniformly. Different countries responded at different speeds. Healthcare systems in Liberia improved faster than Sierra Leone. Community acceptance of isolation varied. The model assumed homogeneity. Reality was heterogeneous.

### The Long Game: 6+ Months Out

Forecast error was higher for forecasts with longer time horizons (29% increase in mean absolute percentage error per week), and forecasts became progressively more inaccurate as they projected dynamics beyond several weeks.

Six-month forecasts are not predictions. They are cautionary tales. They are useful for advocacy—"if we do nothing, here's what happens"—but useless for operational planning.

My predictions extending to January 2027 (7 months out) fall into this category. They are based on sound epidemiological math, but they assume interventions don't meaningfully accelerate, vaccines don't arrive faster than expected, and behavior change happens slowly.

All three assumptions could be wrong.

## The Accuracy of My Six Predictions: A Critical Review

### Prediction 1: Europe Cases Exceed Uganda by September
**Confidence**: 75% | **Reasoning**: Solid | **Major Risk**: Response speed

**Why it's likely**: The 2014–2016 outbreak spread to 7 additional countries (Italy, Mali, Nigeria, Senegal, Spain, UK, USA), with secondary infections in Italy, Mali, Nigeria, and USA. If Bundibugyo reaches major hubs (Kinshasa, Goma, Kampala), European medical tourism and international referral networks will carry cases. Uganda's healthcare system is stronger than Central African hubs, and cases will naturally filter toward better-equipped hospitals. Europe has better detection (it's a wealthy region with strong surveillance). This prediction assumes no dramatic intervention acceleration.

**How it could fail**: If response marshals 70%+ isolation by July (West Africa took until September–October to achieve this), the exponential curve breaks. Uganda would contain while Europe remains low.

### Prediction 2: U.S. Healthcare Worker Death by October
**Confidence**: 45% | **Reasoning**: Plausible but optimistic | **Major Risk**: Diagnostic speed in U.S. hospitals

**Why it's uncertain**: The U.S. has surveillance for this. Missed diagnosis in a U.S. ED is rare because Ebola is rare *and* because triage in American hospitals includes travel history. The West Africa outbreak had 4 cases in the U.S.; none resulted in secondary transmission. Between December 2013 and April 2016, cases were reported from Italy, Mali, Nigeria, Senegal, Spain, the UK, and USA, with secondary infections occurring in Italy, Mali, Nigeria, and USA. Secondary transmission in the U.S. did not occur. The healthcare system worked.

**However**, if this is a severe healthcare crisis—not a contained curiosity—protocols change. Fear accelerates. Mistakes happen. I set this at 45% because it's plausible but contradicts historical U.S. resilience.

### Prediction 3: Goma Crosses 1,000 Cases by December
**Confidence**: 60% | **Reasoning**: Depends entirely on intervention speed | **Major Risk**: Unknown

**Why it's plausible**: Goma is a city of 1.4 million. Ituri is a commercial and migratory hub with proximity to Uganda and South Sudan, increasing the risk of regional exportation and cross-border transmission. Goma, a city in North Kivu Province, had its first confirmed case on May 17 after a woman traveled from Ituri. If Goma becomes a secondary epicenter, exponential growth in a dense urban area is the default trajectory. One case on May 17 → dozens by July → hundreds by September → thousands by November. The math works.

**Why it could fail**: International response focuses on Goma specifically. Vaccination campaigns deploy faster than expected. Isolation rates spike to 80%+ (still higher than achieved in West Africa 2014 until October). Movement restrictions imposed.

### Prediction 4: Nosocomial Outbreak Outside Africa by January
**Confidence**: 50% | **Reasoning**: Probability-based, not mechanistic | **Major Risk**: Depends on raw case numbers**

**The logic**: If 100 infected travelers attempt entry to U.S./Europe over 7 months, and screening catches 95%, five slip through. If one reaches hospitalization before diagnosis, secondary transmission is mathematically probable in a standard isolation room.

**The risk**: Screening might catch 99% (not 95%). Or the 5% who slip through might be asymptomatic or isolated before exposure to HCWs. Or hospitals might pre-emptively use higher precautions (post-COVID protocols are now normalized). The actual probability is probably 20–40%, not 50%.

### Prediction 5: Global Deaths Exceed 5,000 by January
**Confidence**: 70% | **Reasoning**: Conservative | **Major Risk**: Underestimating confirmed deaths

**Why it's likely**: Even with intervention, 676 confirmed cases on June 8 → assuming 30% growth per week (less aggressive than current 50%) → roughly 2,000–2,500 confirmed by January. Deaths at 20% CFR = 400–500 confirmed deaths. But the DRC death data lags by weeks. Unconfirmed deaths in the community are 3–5× confirmed deaths. A total of 11,310 deaths was recorded, but the true toll was certainly greater. By January, the cumulative toll (confirmed + highly probable) will exceed 5,000.

**However**, if intervention is aggressive, this could be wrong. If isolation hits 70% by August and stays there, the outbreak plateaus. Deaths might reach 3,000–4,000, not 5,000.

### Prediction 6: First Vaccine Trial Fails to Stop Transmission
**Confidence**: 85% | **Reasoning**: Timeline physics | **Major Risk**: None (this is nearly certain)**

**Why it's airtight**: Vaccine trials cannot compress beyond 12–16 weeks for safety data. Manufacturing is 6–8 weeks. Distribution is 4–6 weeks. The outbreak's current trajectory reaches peak in October–November. A vaccine arriving in March 2027 cannot affect the 2026 arc. This prediction is not speculative; it is temporal. The vaccine will be too late for this outbreak, though not too late for the next one.

## First-Principles Reassessment: What the Math Actually Says

### The Fundamental Variables

In any epidemic, the trajectory depends on exactly five parameters:

1. **R (reproduction number)**: How many people each case infects. For Bundibugyo, estimated at 1.5–2.5, similar to West Africa.
2. **Generation time**: How fast infections occur (2–5 week incubation → 2–3 weeks from infection to infectiousness).
3. **Intervention effect**: What percentage of cases are isolated, and when.
4. **Population density**: How close people are; affects transmission probability.
5. **Healthcare collapse threshold**: The point at which the system can no longer diagnose or isolate new cases.

Current state:
- **R**: ~1.8 (estimated from doubling time)
- **Generation time**: 3 weeks (standard for Ebola)
- **Intervention effect**: <30% isolation (based on DRC data); rising slowly
- **Population density**: Heterogeneous (rural Ituri = low; Goma = high)
- **Healthcare collapse threshold**: Possibly reached in rural zones; not yet in regional hubs

### The Sensitivity Analysis

If isolation reaches 50% by August: outbreak peaks in October at 3,000–4,000 total cases.

If isolation stays <30% until September: outbreak peaks in December at 8,000–12,000 total cases.

If a vaccine deploys rapidly (unlikely) and reaches 30% population coverage by November: peak delays, total cases rise to 5,000–7,000, but deaths fall.

If no intervention accelerates: outbreak follows current trajectory → 10,000+ cases by January 2027, with 2,000+ deaths.

**My predictions assume the middle path**: intervention accelerates to 40–50% by September, curbing but not stopping exponential growth. This puts total cases at 4,000–6,000 by January, with 800–1,200 deaths confirmed, and 2,500–5,000+ when including unconfirmed deaths.

## The Historical Lesson: Why Predictions Fail

The 2014 CDC forecast was mathematically sound but behaviorally naive. It assumed:
- Interventions would deploy at a constant rate.
- Behavior wouldn't change absent formal interventions.
- All regions would respond identically.

**None of these held.**

The 2014–2016 Ebola outbreak highlights the need for better understanding of how increasing awareness of severe infections within a community decreases transmissibility even in the absence of specific interventions.

People changed behavior not because governments told them to, but because they watched their neighbors die. Healthcare workers took precautions even before official PPE arrived. Communities rejected burials that violated customs. These informal interventions cut transmission faster than any model predicted.

### Will This Happen Again?

Partially. Bundibugyo knowledge is higher now than in 2014. There is less surprise. But Bundibugyo is also more deadly *and* more contained (no vaccine, but also less press, less international attention, no West African multinational context).

The wild card: **Do low-income Central African countries have the will to isolate 70% of cases?** In West Africa 2014, isolation was slow because:
- Hospitals were disease vectors (patients went to hospitals, caught Ebola, died, spread to families).
- Burial practices conflicted with safe handling.
- Distrust of foreign intervention was high.
- Economic pressure to seek income was urgent.

All five factors still exist in the DRC.

### The Meta-Prediction: What Actually Matters
**The true outcome will be determined not by exponential math, but by institutional behavior.**

If the DRC's government, WHO, and international donors decide to respond like 2018–2020 (with vaccine/therapeutics), outbreak caps at 3,000–5,000 cases.

If they respond like West Africa 2014 (slowly, with political hesitation), outbreak reaches 8,000–15,000 cases.

If they fail to respond at all (unlikely but conceivable), outbreak could exceed 20,000 cases before international intervention forces action.

The mathematics is a ceiling, not a prediction. Reality depends on choice.

## The Uncomfortable Corollary: Better Data, Worse Forecasts

Modern forecasting is more sophisticated than 2014. We have better models. More data. Real-time surveillance.

And yet: Although early Ebola predictive models were useful as advocacy tools, their perceived scientific accuracy remains debated.

The problem is that better models don't help if the input variables (government response, community behavior, healthcare capacity) are unknowns. We are trying to predict human institutions, not viral transmission.

Viral transmission is predictable. Human response is not.

My six predictions are sound epidemiology paired with assumed institutional behavior. The epidemiology will hold. The institutions? That is where I expect to be wrong.
